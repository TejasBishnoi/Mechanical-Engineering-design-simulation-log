**[Mesh Information]{.underline}**

First of all, we should understand what Mesh actually is.

In a FEA(Finite Element Analysis), a mesh is the process of dividing a
geometry into a large number of smaller, discrete elements. These
elements are connected at points called nodes. The equations of the
"Physical Problem" are solved at these nodes. Allowing the software(in
this case, SolidWorks Simulations) to \~ approximate how the model will
behave under the load.

So, in our case.

A solid tetrahedral mesh was used for the analysis and generated using
the default meshing settings in SolidWorks. The mesh contained around
9,600 elements and 17,900 nodes. Most of the elements had a good shape,
with more than 97% having a low aspect ratio and no visibly distorted
elements. The mesh was kept reasonably fine so that the results would be
accurate enough while still keeping the simulation time low. Since the
main aim of this study was to understand the simulation process and
workflow, a detailed mesh convergence study was not performed, as it was
a learning journey only.

**So, what are all these titles:**

*[Automatic Transition]{.underline}*: Automatic transition is the option
that controls gradual element size change.

*[Include Mesh Auto Loops:]{.underline}* Auto loops if turned on,
refines mesh around holes and edges automatically.

*[Jacobian Points for high quality mesh]{.underline}*: its checks for
element distortion, in this case 16 points is good and professional

*[Element Size]{.underline}*: This is the global average element edge
length. The analogy is, larger element size equals to faster solve and
less accurate result. And For Finer element size equals to Slower Solve
and greater accuracy.

*[Tolerance]{.underline}*: Tolerance controls how accurately the mesh
follows the model.

*[Nodes]{.underline}*: Nodes are the points at which the displacement is
calculated. More nodes will mean more equations which in result means
more accuracy.

*[Element]{.underline}*: An Element is the smallest finite volume over
which the governing equations are solved.

*[Aspect ratio]{.underline}*: aspect ratio is the ratio of how stretched
an element is.

**What are all these terms:**

*[Reaction Forces]{.underline}*: Forces generated at constraints(aka
fixtures) to satisfy equilibrium.

*[Reaction Moment]{.underline}*: Moment (Rotation generated by a force
along a axis) generated at fixtures to prevent rotation.

*[Freebody Forces]{.underline}*: Equivalent forces acting on a isolated
part of the model.

*[Freebody moment]{.underline}*: Equivalent torque acting on the
isolated body or face.

**[What we actually solved for:]{.underline}**

> ***[Stress]{.underline}**:* Stress is the internal resistance force
> per unit area that develops inside material when an external load is
> applied.\
> $$\sigma = \frac{A}{F}$$
>
> In structural analysis, stress represent how the applied load are
> distributed within the material in the study, Von Mises Stress is used
> as the primary stress measurement. Regions of high stress typically
> occurs near geometric discontinuities such as fillet, holes and sharp
> corners.
>
> ***[Strain]{.underline}***: Strain is the measure of deformation per
> unit length and describes how much a material deforms under load.
>
> â€‹ $\varepsilon\  = \ \frac{\mathrm{\Delta}L}{L}$
>
> Strain is dimensionless quantity. It is directly related to the
> Hooke's Law for linear elastic behaviour. In this analysis, strain
> indicates the extent of elastic deformation and helps identify areas
> experiencing significant material deformation.
>
> ***[Displacement]{.underline}***: It represents the total movement of
> a point or region of the model from its original position due to
> applied loads.
